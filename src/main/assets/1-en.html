The Osage orange (Maclura pomifera, Schneider), planted in 1935, is remarkable for its three trunks clump. It belongs to the family Moraceae, native to North America.

In 2011, this tree was 13 meters tall with trunks of 1.30 meters in circumference.

Originally from South East United States of America. Introduced in France in 1812 for its foliage near Mulberry, who were believed to feed the silkworms in areas too cold for the cultivation of mulberry trees.

Only female plants bear fruit: dioecious species. Also called "bois d'arc" because Native Americans used the young branches to make bows.

This tree gives fruit, "oranges" inedible, halved, free latex very popular among American Indians and used them for making makeup and textile dyeing. Sometimes preferred var. Inermis, thornless.

Maclura, kind dedicated to American geologist William Maclure (1763-1840), by fellow botanist who discovered the Nuttal M.pomifera / aurantiaca. Osage, because it bears the name of the Sioux tribe in the territory where it was found.
